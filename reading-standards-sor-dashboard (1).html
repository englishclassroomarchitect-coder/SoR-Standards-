What This Document Does
Translating the 2024 English SOL through a science of reading lens for secondary curriculum design

The 2024 English Standards of Learning were revised in alignment with the Virginia Literacy Act, which is built on science of reading principles. At the elementary level, this translation is well underway — phonics programs, decodable texts, structured literacy curricula. This document extends that translation to the secondary level for our department.

The science of reading tells us that reading comprehension is the product of word recognition and language comprehension. At the high school level, word recognition becomes morphological awareness — understanding how words are built from meaningful parts. Language comprehension depends on vocabulary depth, background knowledge, text structure knowledge, and verbal reasoning. These aren't add-ons. They're what determine whether a student can make meaning from complex text.

The Problem This Addresses

The standards are written grade by grade, and the cognitive work is cumulative. Reading the standards at a single grade level shows what students should be able to do that year. Reading them across grade levels shows the trajectory — where students need to end up and what foundations support that arrival. This document reads the standards as trajectories, showing the full arc from where students need to arrive in 11th grade back through what begins in 9th.

The goal is to help each teacher see the depth available in their own grade level's standard, informed by the full arc. Seeing where the progression is going can reveal opportunities within a standard that might not be visible when reading it in isolation.

SoR: Word Structure
Morphology as a Reading Tool
Teaching students to see words as built from meaningful parts with traceable histories
Test Evidence
Practice Test Q14
"In this sentence, the word effaced means —"
The move: The student uses surrounding context AND word structure to determine meaning of an unfamiliar word. A student who recognizes the root FACE/FACERE (to make/to do) and the prefix EF- (out) has a structural strategy beyond context clues alone.
Practice Test Q7
"The author's word choice in this sentence highlights the idea that —"
The move: The student analyzes how specific word choices ("facade had evaporated") create meaning. The word "facade" — from Italian facciata, from FACE (an outward appearance) — isn't just a synonym for "act." Its etymology carries the specific connotation of a building's front, an exterior that hides what's behind it. The word choice IS the characterization.
The Concept

Morphology instruction teaches students that words are built from parts (roots, prefixes, suffixes) and that those parts carry consistent meaning across word families. At the secondary level, this isn't phonics — it's the grown-up version of decoding. A student who knows VIV- means "life" can work with "vivify" even without strong surrounding context. A student who knows MONERE means "to warn" reads "monster" differently in Frankenstein.

Morphology becomes a reading tool when students see that a word's history is part of what gives it meaning in a specific text. The etymology of a word is an interpretive resource.

The Instructional Guide at all three levels says vocabulary instruction should emphasize "roots, affixes, and etymology" and that teachers should "provide students time to explore and build background knowledge around the historical, social, and cultural contexts — especially when they have a substantial impact on author's vocabulary." Together, etymology and cultural context are the tools for teaching morphology as a reading tool. When these standards are taught in connection with each other, they become more powerful than when taught separately.

Cognitive Handoff — What Students Should Be Able to Do

Leaving 9th Grade

Recognize that words are built from parts and that those parts carry consistent meaning across different words. Use morphological structure as one strategy for determining unfamiliar word meaning. Begin to see that knowing where a word comes from sometimes changes how you read it in a text.

This is the noticing phase. Students develop the habit of looking inside words. They don't need to memorize root lists. They need enough examples that "words are built from parts" becomes an automatic perceptual habit.

Leaving 10th Grade

Track morphological patterns across a text — noticing when the same root shows up in multiple words and asking what that pattern reveals. Use etymology not just to define unfamiliar words but to deepen interpretation of familiar ones. Connect a word's root to the thematic work of the text.

This is the tracking and connecting phase. Students move from seeing individual words as built from parts to seeing patterns of related words across a text. The morpheme becomes a lens for reading.

Leaving 11th Grade

Analyze why an author chose this word and not a synonym — using morphological knowledge to articulate the difference. Hold multiple words with similar surface meanings and explain the distinct weight each carries. Use the history of a word as evidence in literary analysis.

This is the arguing with evidence from language itself phase. Morphological knowledge becomes a source of analytical evidence.

In Our Texts

9th Grade

Romeo & Juliet

The root FAT- (from Latin fatum: "that which has been spoken") appears across the play — "fatal," "fate," "fateful." Students who learn that fate in its original Latin sense means something spoken into existence read the Prologue differently: it doesn't just predict the ending, it performs it. The act of speaking the Prologue is the act of sealing the fate. The Prologue's structure (telling the audience the ending) and its language (FAT- saturated) are making the same argument.

9th Grade

The Odyssey

The shift from Latin to Greek roots is itself a teaching opportunity. NOSTOS (return home) gives students "nostalgia" — literally the pain of returning. For students who think nostalgia means "happy memories," the etymology reframes the entire poem. Odysseus's journey isn't a fun adventure — it's a painful ache to get home. One root, one minute, more interpretive work than a week of plot summary.

10th Grade

Macbeth & Frankenstein

VOC- (voice, call) generates "equivocation," "invocation," and "provoke" in Macbeth. Students who track VOC- across the play trace how its central concern — language that conceals rather than reveals — is built into the word choices themselves. MONERE (to warn) connects "monster," "demonstrate," and "premonition" in Frankenstein. If a monster is a warning sign, what is the creature warning about? The bridge root MORT- appears in both texts ("mortal," "murder" in Macbeth; "mortal," "immortal," "mortality" in Frankenstein), so students who tracked death-language in one text arrive at the other already sensitized.

11th Grade

The Crucible

Legal and religious vocabulary carries double meanings morphology makes visible. "Conviction" from CON- + VINCERE (to conquer completely) means both a legal judgment and a deeply held belief. That double root is the play's entire tension: personal convictions produce legal convictions. A student who can articulate that isn't doing vocabulary — they're doing literary analysis using language as evidence.

Design Questions for Unit Planning

9th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Which words in this text have roots that would surprise students? You're looking for 2–3 moments where knowing the etymology visibly changes how students read a word they thought they already understood. Not a root list — a few high-impact moments.

Where in the unit does the etymological reveal do the most interpretive work? Timing matters. Introducing FAT- (fatum = "that which has been spoken") before students read the Prologue of Romeo & Juliet changes their entire experience of that moment. The same information after Act 3 is trivia.

How will students encounter the idea that words are built from parts — through discovery or instruction? Either can work, but both should be connected to the text, not taught as a standalone vocabulary lesson.

Depth anchor: Students learn that "nostalgia" comes from NOSTOS (return home) + ALGOS (pain) and discuss how "the pain of returning" changes their understanding of why the Odyssey isn't an adventure story. One root, one conversation, a reframing of the entire text.

10th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Which roots recur across the text, and what pattern does the recurrence create? A root that appears once is a vocabulary word. A root that appears five times across three scenes is a pattern — and the pattern is an argument the text is making at the level of language.

Are there bridge roots connecting to what students encountered last year? If MORT- appeared in 9th grade and appears again now, that's not repetition — it's accumulation. Students who tracked death-language before arrive already sensitized.

Where in the unit will students track morpheme patterns themselves, rather than receiving them from you? The cognitive shift at 10th grade is from "the teacher shows me something surprising about a word" to "I notice a pattern in how the author uses words with shared roots."

Depth anchor: Students track VOC- (voice/call) across Macbeth — equivocation, invocation, provoke — and write about how the play's central concern (language that conceals) is built into the word choices themselves. They're not doing vocabulary. They're using morphology as a lens for analyzing the text's architecture.

11th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Where does the text offer moments where two near-synonyms do different work? This is where the nuance standard (11.RV.1.D) lives in practice. Look for moments where the author's word choice matters precisely because a different word — one that means almost the same thing — would change the effect.

How will students use morphological knowledge as evidence in analytical writing? By 11th grade, the goal is for students to argue that a word choice matters BECAUSE of where the word comes from — not as a fun fact but as part of a claim about what the author is doing.

Where can students practice articulating shade — the difference between words that share a denotation? This needs to happen in discussion before it appears in writing. Students need to hear themselves say "those words mean the same thing but feel different" before they can explain why.

Depth anchor: Students analyze "conviction" in The Crucible — from CON- + VINCERE (to conquer completely) — and argue that the word's double meaning (legal judgment + deeply held belief) IS the play's central tension. The shared etymology isn't decoration. It's evidence for an interpretive claim.
SoR: Vocabulary Depth
Reading Word Meaning in Context
Understanding what a word does here that it wouldn't do somewhere else
Test Evidence
Practice Test Q1
"In paragraph 1, the expression 'rhythm of their lives' suggests that people's lives were filled with —"
The move: Interpret a figurative/connotative expression for what it implies beyond its literal meaning. This isn't vocabulary-as-definition — it's reading connotation and implication inside a specific sentence.
Practice Test Q21
"In line 21, the speaker uses the word 'lush' when describing the patch of grass to —"
The move: Analyze why a specific word matters in this specific context. The student has to see the contrast — "lush" against a desolate alley — and articulate what the word choice accomplishes that a different word wouldn't.

Vocabulary questions on this test cluster into three distinct types: (1) use context to determine unfamiliar word meaning, (2) interpret figurative/connotative language for what it implies, and (3) analyze why a specific word choice matters in this spot. These are three different cognitive tasks that all live under "vocabulary." Together they account for roughly 22–26% of the practice set.

The Concept

Connotation is not a fixed property of a word — it's the relationship between the word and everything around it. "Cold" in a weather report is denotative. "Cold" describing a person is connotative. "Cold" describing a person who just lost a child is doing something entirely different than "cold" describing a villain. The connotation lives in the context.

This means even the 9th grade standard — "discriminate between connotative and denotative meanings" — has more depth available than it might appear. The Understanding the Standards document defines connotation as "the emotional or social meaning/implication of the word" and gives "cool" as the example. Interpreting connotation fully requires reading the word inside its moment in a specific text — which is a reading skill that develops over time.

Standards — What the Progression Says

Grade 9

Discriminate

Discriminate between the connotative and denotative meanings and interpret the connotation(s).

9.RV.1.D

Grade 10

Discriminate

Discriminate between the connotative and denotative meanings and interpret the connotation(s).

10.RV.1.D

Grade 11

Analyze nuances

Analyze the nuances in the meaning of words with similar denotations (e.g., clever, cunning, brainy).

11.RV.1.D

The shift from grades 9–10 to 11 is significant. For two years, students practice distinguishing connotation from denotation — a binary distinction. The 11th grade standard asks them to analyze nuances among words that share a denotation, which is a different perceptual skill. "What makes 'cunning' different from 'clever' when they technically mean the same thing?" requires sensitivity to shade that benefits from being developed before 11th grade, even though the standard formally names it there.

Cognitive Handoff

Leaving 9th Grade

Distinguish between what a word literally means and what it implies in context. Recognize that the same word can carry different weight depending on where it appears. Begin noticing that authors choose specific words deliberately.

What would change if the author had written "passionate" instead of "ardent"? "Obsessive" instead of "dedicated"?

Questions like this exceed the 9th grade standard but build the shade-sensitivity that 11th grade depends on.

Leaving 10th Grade

Analyze how a word's connotation functions in a specific moment — what it does to characterization, tone, or theme. Connect word-level choices to larger patterns of meaning. See that connotation shifts depending on context — the same word does different work in different moments of the same text.

Look at these three words the author uses to describe power. They all mean something similar — what's different about each one? What does the choice of this word in this moment tell you?

Leaving 11th Grade

Hold multiple words with similar denotations and articulate the distinct connotative, historical, or rhetorical weight each carries. Use word choice analysis as evidence in argument — arguing that an author's selection of this word over its synonyms reveals something about intent, perspective, or strategy.

Both writers argue for reform, but one uses "persistent" and the other uses "unrelenting." What's the difference in how they want to be perceived?
In Our Texts

9th Grade

Romeo & Juliet

Romeo's language shifts dramatically between his infatuation with Rosaline (Petrarchan clichés — "feather of lead," "bright smoke," "cold fire") and his encounter with Juliet (direct, specific, sensory). A 9th grader can notice the shift and describe what's different. That's the entry point: the same character uses language differently in two contexts, and the difference tells you something about what's real versus performed.

10th Grade

Frankenstein

Victor describes the Creature with language of disgust — "wretch," "daemon," "abortion." The Creature describes himself with language of longing — "benevolent," "sympathies," "affections." The same being, described by two narrators, in two completely different connotative registers. Students who notice this are reading unreliable narration through word choice.

11th Grade

The Great Gatsby

Fitzgerald uses "orgastic" (his coinage) in the final passage rather than any existing word. Nick's vocabulary throughout — "incorruptible dream," "foul dust," "gorgeous" applied to Gatsby — carries evaluative weight that reveals Nick's own position as much as Gatsby's character. A student who can analyze what Nick's word choices reveal about Nick (not just about Gatsby) is doing the 11th grade work.

Design Questions for Unit Planning

9th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Where in this text does word choice reveal character, and would students notice without prompting? Often the most powerful connotation work comes from moments students would read past — a word that seems ordinary until you ask "why this word and not another?"

Are there moments where the same word carries different weight in different scenes? A word that means one thing in Act 1 and something different in Act 5 teaches connotation as context-dependent better than any definition exercise.

How will students experience the shift from "what does this word mean" to "what does this word do here"? That shift is the bridge between vocabulary instruction and literary analysis. It doesn't happen automatically — it needs a question that requires it.

Depth anchor: Students compare Romeo's language about Rosaline (Petrarchan clichés: "feather of lead," "bright smoke") with his language about Juliet (direct, specific, sensory). They describe what the shift tells them about what's real versus performed — without the teacher naming the conclusion. The connotative register IS the characterization.

10th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Where do different narrators or characters describe the same thing in different connotative registers? This is where connotation stops being about individual word meaning and becomes about perspective — two people looking at the same thing and choosing different words to describe it.

Are there moments where a word's connotation shifts across the text? A word that starts neutral and accumulates negative weight (or vice versa) teaches students that connotation is built through context, not fixed in the dictionary.

How will students practice naming what a word choice does to tone, characterization, or theme — explaining the mechanism? The 10th grade move is toward mechanism: "this word makes the reader distrust the narrator because..."

Depth anchor: Students examine how Victor describes the Creature ("wretch," "daemon," "abortion") versus how the Creature describes himself ("benevolent," "sympathies," "affections"). The same being, two connotative registers. Students articulate what each register reveals about the speaker — which is unreliable narration read through word choice.

11th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Where does the author's word choice reveal the author's position as much as the subject's? By 11th grade, students should see that how someone describes something tells you about the describer, not just the described.

Where can students practice the "why this word and not its synonym" move in discussion before it appears in writing? Nuance analysis is a verbal skill before it's a written one. Students need to hear themselves make fine distinctions out loud.

How will students use word choice analysis as evidence in argument — not as a separate vocabulary observation but integrated into a claim about the text?

Depth anchor: Students analyze Nick's vocabulary in Gatsby — "incorruptible dream," "foul dust," "gorgeous" applied to Gatsby — and argue that Nick's word choices reveal more about Nick's own investment in the dream than about Gatsby's character. The narrator's vocabulary IS the evidence for a claim about the narrator.
SoR: Background Knowledge
Background Knowledge as a Reading Tool
What the reader knows about the world the text came from determines what they can see in the text
Test Evidence
Practice Test Q8
"Why does the author include the flashback in paragraph 9?"
The move: The passage ("The Intersection") is set during WWII. The flashback in paragraph 9 describes how Pearl Harbor changed Louise's life. A student who doesn't know what Pearl Harbor was, what the draft meant, or why young women went to work in factories can still identify that this is a flashback — but they can't fully analyze what it accomplishes, because the historical weight of the moment is invisible to them. The structural question is also a background knowledge question.
Practice Test Q5
"Based on information in the selection, which statement represents the author's opinion?"
The move: Distinguishing fact from opinion requires schema — knowing enough about a topic to recognize when a claim is verifiable versus evaluative. A student with no background knowledge about timekeeping can't judge whether "life in ancient times was simpler" is fact or opinion. Background knowledge is what makes critical reading possible.
The Concept

Background knowledge is the most frequently referenced element across the standards and guidance documents. The guidance often frames it as something the teacher provides — pre-teach, pre-assess, fill gaps. There is also an opportunity to frame it as something students learn to build for themselves over time.

Background knowledge functions as a reading practice — a set of questions students can learn to ask about any text: What world did this come from? What was the author responding to? What did the original audience know that I don't? And how do the answers change what I see in the text?

The 11th grade Understanding the Standards document says it directly: "By eleventh grade, students need to already understand that no work of literature exists without context." Already. Before they get there.

Cognitive Handoff

Leaving 9th Grade

Recognize that a text comes from somewhere — that the author's world shaped what they wrote. Describe, when given context, how that context changes what they see. Begin asking "what did the audience know that I don't?" as a reading question.

This is the recognizing that context matters phase. The teacher's role is to create moments where context visibly changes interpretation — so students learn to expect and eventually seek out those moments.

Why is Juliet's father so furious? What are we missing about this world that would make his reaction make sense?

Leaving 10th Grade

Actively investigate a text's context through reading and research. Explain how a specific piece of context changes how the text works — not just that context matters, but what it does to form, style, character, or POV. Notice gaps in understanding as signals to seek context.

This is the building and using context independently phase. Students move from receiving background knowledge to constructing it.

Frankenstein was published anonymously in 1818. Mary Shelley added her name and revised the text in 1831. What changed, and what does that tell us about what she could and couldn't say as a woman in each moment?

Leaving 11th Grade

Use context as evidence in analytical argument. Hold multiple contexts simultaneously — when the text was written, when it's set, what the author was responding to — and articulate how those layers interact. Evaluate how an author's position shapes what they can see and what they're blind to.

This is the context as analytical argument phase.

The Crucible is set in 1692 but written in 1953. What did Miller change from the historical record, and what does each change accomplish for his argument about McCarthy?
In Our Texts

9th Grade

The Odyssey

The concept of xenia (guest-friendship) governs virtually every scene. The Cyclops isn't just a monster — he's a bad host, which in the Greek world violates divine law. Telemachus's journey in Books 1–4 is a tour of proper hospitality. When students learn xenia, the entire poem reorganizes around a framework they couldn't see before. That's the experience the teacher is designing for: the moment when context visibly transforms the text.

10th Grade

Macbeth

The play was written for James I — a Scottish king on the English throne who was fascinated by witchcraft and traced his lineage to Banquo. Students who research James I can explain why the witches' prophecy about Banquo's descendants matters politically, not just narratively. The play is simultaneously a drama and a political document — and students who can articulate that are doing 10.RL.3.A.

11th Grade

The Crucible

The Crucible demands students hold two contexts simultaneously. Salem is Salem, and Salem is also McCarthy's America. Miller's choices — which historical details to include, which to invent, which to alter — become visible when students ask: "Why did Miller change this? What does the change accomplish for his argument?" Background knowledge here becomes the foundation for evaluating an author's rhetorical strategy.

Design Questions for Unit Planning

9th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

What's the one piece of context that, when students learn it, visibly transforms how they read the text? You're designing for the "oh" moment — the point where a student's confusion resolves because they now know something about the world the text came from. Identify that moment before the unit starts.

Where will students feel the gap between their world and the text's world — and how will that gap become a question rather than a barrier? Confusion is the signal, not the problem. The design move is making sure students have a way to turn "this doesn't make sense" into "what don't I know?"

How much context will you provide versus how much will students encounter through reading? Some context must be pre-taught (you can't discover xenia from the text alone). But every piece of teacher-provided context should be followed by a return to the text: "Now read this scene again knowing that. What do you see now?"

Depth anchor: Students learn xenia (Greek guest-friendship as divine law) and then re-read the Cyclops scene. They can now see that Polyphemus isn't just dangerous — he's a violator of the most sacred social code in the Greek world. The "oh" is visible. Students articulate what they see now that they couldn't see before. That meta-awareness — "context changed my reading" — is the skill being built.

10th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

What are the layers of context for this text, and which ones are most productive for analysis? Most texts have multiple relevant contexts (biographical, historical, scientific, literary, political). Not all are equally useful. Choose the 1–2 that most directly change how students read specific passages.

Where in the unit will students build context themselves through research or reading? The 10th grade shift is toward student-constructed context. This means building in time and structure for investigation.

How will students practice explaining what context does to the text — how knowing it changes interpretation? "This was written in 1818" is information. "Knowing this was written in 1818 changes how I read Victor's confidence in science because..." is analysis. Where does the unit ask for that second move?

Depth anchor: Students research galvanism and the real experiments of the early 1800s, then re-read Victor's description of his studies. They write about how knowing the science was real (not fictional) changes whether Victor seems visionary or reckless. Context isn't backdrop — it's an interpretive lever that produces different readings depending on which context you apply.

11th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Does this text require students to hold multiple contexts simultaneously, and how will you scaffold that? Some texts (The Crucible) demand dual context — when it was written AND when it's set AND what the author was responding to. Students need structure for holding these layers, not just exposure to them.

Where will students use context as evidence in argument — as the substance of their analytical claim, appearing in body paragraphs?

How will students evaluate what an author's position allows them to see and what it makes them blind to? This is the most sophisticated context move — recognizing that every author writes from somewhere, and that "somewhere" has limits as well as advantages.

Depth anchor: Students identify three historical details Miller changed from the Salem trial records and argue what each change accomplishes for his argument about McCarthyism. They analyze the gap between historical record and dramatic invention as evidence of authorial strategy. The historical research is the literary analysis.

Test Evidence
Practice Test Q8
"Why does the author include the flashback in paragraph 9?"
The move: Recognize a structural choice (flashback) and analyze what it accomplishes narratively. This isn't identification — it's purpose analysis. The student must explain what the flashback does for the story that a chronological telling wouldn't.

"The author organizes information in paragraph 1 using explanation most likely to —"

The move: Analyze how a rhetorical choice (the question form itself) reveals something about the speaker's inner life. The structure of the sentence IS the meaning.

Author's structural purpose questions account for roughly 30% of the practice set — the single largest cluster. This is the most heavily tested cognitive move.

The Concept

Text structure at the secondary level includes the choices an author makes about how to build a text — who tells it, where it starts, what the reader knows that the characters don't, whether the text acknowledges its own existence as a text. Each of these choices shapes meaning.

Key structural concepts students need across 9–11:

In medias res — starting in the middle. What does the reader gain or lose by entering the story here instead of at the beginning?

Frame and nested narration — a story containing other stories. The containing structure changes the meaning of what's contained.

Unreliable narration — every narrator has a position, an interest, a reason for telling the story their way. Not just "this narrator is lying" but "what does this narrator's version reveal and conceal?"

Dramatic irony — the audience knows something the characters don't, created by structural choices (prologues, soliloquies, retrospective narration). The gap between audience knowledge and character knowledge IS the experience of reading.

Allegory / double context — a text operating on two levels simultaneously. The surface narrative and the argument underneath.

Direct address / breaking the frame — when the text acknowledges its audience or its own status as a text.

Grade 9

Cognitive Handoff

Leaving 9th Grade

Recognize that how a story is told is a choice, and that the choice changes what you experience as a reader. Name structural features (in medias res, prologue, dramatic irony) not as labels but as choices with effects.

The Prologue tells you Romeo and Juliet will die. Why would Shakespeare do that? What changes about your experience of watching the play when you already know the ending?

Leaving 10th Grade

Analyze what structural choices accomplish — not just that they exist but what they do to the reader's relationship to the characters, the information, and the themes. Consider alternatives: what would change if the text were structured differently?

You never hear the Creature's story without it passing through Victor's narration, which passes through Walton's letters. What does that nesting do? Whose version do you trust, and why does the structure make that question unavoidable?

Leaving 11th Grade

Evaluate structural choices — make and defend a judgment about whether a choice works. Hold multiple structural layers simultaneously and articulate how they interact.

Miller inserts historical essays directly into The Crucible, breaking the play's dramatic frame. Does that strengthen or weaken his argument? What does he gain by reminding you this is about history, and what does he lose by breaking the illusion?
In Our Texts

9th Grade

Romeo & Juliet + The Odyssey

Romeo & Juliet's Prologue is one moment doing three things simultaneously: structurally, it tells you the ending (creating dramatic irony for the entire play). Linguistically, it's saturated with FAT- — "fatal loins," "death-mark'd love" — and if students know fatum means "that which has been spoken," the Prologue is performing the fate it describes. Contextually, Elizabethan audiences understood spoken destiny as a real force. Structure, morphology, and background knowledge converge on fourteen lines. The Odyssey opens in medias res and then has the hero narrate his own journey to people who are deciding whether to help him. He's an interested narrator. The nonlinear structure isn't a stylistic flourish — it's a characterization move.

10th Grade

Macbeth + Frankenstein

Macbeth's soliloquies give the audience access to his interior that no other character has. The audience becomes the only entity that knows both what Macbeth does and what he thinks about it — which makes the audience complicit. Frankenstein's nested frame (Walton → Victor → Creature) means every voice is filtered through at least one other. The structure IS the argument about whose story gets told and who controls the narrative.

11th Grade

The Great Gatsby + The Crucible

Nick narrates Gatsby retrospectively — reconstructing events he only partially understood, choosing what to include and reveal. The structure makes Nick's judgment inseparable from the story itself. The Crucible operates as allegory (Salem = McCarthy's America), includes Miller's direct-address historical essays that break dramatic frame, and requires students to hold the gap between 1692 and 1953 as the space where the argument lives. This is the most structurally complex text in the curriculum because it demands holding multiple layers simultaneously.

Design Questions for Unit Planning

9th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

What structural choice does this text make that's unusual — and would students notice it without being told? Most 9th graders will read right past in medias res, a prologue that reveals the ending, or a hero narrating his own story. The first design move is deciding which structural feature to name and when.

How will you frame structure as a choice rather than a label? The goal isn't "students can identify in medias res." It's "students can articulate what the author gains by starting here instead of at the beginning." Every structural concept should be taught through the question "what does this choice do?" not "what is this called?"

Where can structural awareness and other strands converge on the same moment? The richest teaching moments are where structure, morphology, and context work simultaneously — like the Prologue of Romeo & Juliet doing structure (dramatic irony), language (FAT- roots), and cultural context (spoken fate) in fourteen lines.

Depth anchor: Students read the Odyssey's opening and discuss: "The story doesn't start at the beginning. It starts with Odysseus stuck, and then he tells his own story to people he needs help from. What does that change? What would be different if we just followed him chronologically from Troy?" Students name at least one thing the structure does that a chronological telling wouldn't — that's the concept landing.

10th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

What does this text's structure make possible that a different structure wouldn't? Frankenstein's nesting makes every account suspect. Macbeth's soliloquies make the audience complicit. The focus is on what the structure produces.

Where will students consider the counterfactual — what would change if this text were structured differently? "What would be lost if..." is the single most important bridge question to evaluative thinking. It forces students to hold the existing structure and an imagined alternative simultaneously.

How will students connect this text's structural choices to structural choices they encountered in 9th grade? Odysseus narrating his own story and Victor narrating his own story through Walton are related moves. Students who see the connection aren't starting over — they're deepening a concept they already have.

Depth anchor: Students discuss: "You never hear the Creature's story except through Victor, and you never hear Victor's story except through Walton. Pick one layer of that nesting and argue what it does — what would be different if that layer weren't there?" Students analyze what a specific structural choice accomplishes.

11th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Does this text require students to hold multiple structural layers simultaneously, and how will you scaffold that? The Crucible's allegory (Salem = McCarthy), combined with Miller's direct-address essays and the gap between historical record and dramatic invention, asks students to hold three structural layers at once. That's cognitively demanding and needs scaffolding.

Where will students evaluate whether a structural choice serves or complicates the author's purpose? The 11th grade standard uses the verbs evaluate and critique: "Does this structural choice serve the author's purpose, or does it create tension with it? Defend your position."

How does the narrator's structural position become evidence for an argument about the text? Nick's retrospective narration in Gatsby is evidence that the story is filtered through a specific consciousness with specific investments.

Depth anchor: Students argue for or against: "Miller's historical essays in The Crucible strengthen his argument by reminding the audience this really happened" versus "Miller's historical essays weaken the play by breaking the dramatic immersion that makes the audience feel the characters' fear." Students take a position, cite structural evidence, and defend their evaluation of the author's choice.
SoR: Verbal Reasoning
Inference and Synthesis
Drawing conclusions beyond what's stated and connecting ideas across a text
Test Evidence
Practice Test Q4
"This selection supports all these statements about timekeeping EXCEPT —"
The move: Hold the entire text in mind, evaluate four claims against it, and find the one that's NOT supported. This is inverse evidence gathering — high cognitive load, requiring synthesis across the full passage. The student must check each claim against available evidence and identify the gap.
Practice Test Q13
"How do scientists know that the canals were dry at times?"
The move: Trace an evidence-to-conclusion chain. The text says water plants are absent from canal sediments, and the student must connect that evidence to the conclusion. This is reading for logical reasoning — understanding how evidence works, not just what it says.
Practice Test Q19
"Both selections express the idea that —"
The move: Cross-text synthesis. Hold a literary and an informational text simultaneously and identify a shared abstract idea. This requires moving from surface details to underlying concepts in both texts and finding the intersection.
The Concept

Inference is the act of drawing a conclusion the text supports but doesn't state directly. When students summarize rather than analyze, the gap is often about knowledge — they may be missing the vocabulary, background, or structural awareness that would let them see what's beneath the surface.

This is the strand where the science of reading insight matters most: inference depends on everything else. A student can't infer if they don't know the words (vocabulary), don't know the world (background knowledge), and can't see the structure (text structure). Inference is where the other strands converge.

Standards — What the Progression Says

Grade 9

Explain & Distinguish

Explain stated or implied themes. Distinguish among facts, reasoned judgments, and speculation. Draw evidence from texts to support claims and inferences.

9.RL.1.A + 9.RI.1.C + 9.DSR.C

Grade 10

Analyze & Evaluate

Analyze development of universal themes. Evaluate how different authors shape viewpoints using facts, opinions, and reasoning.

10.RL.1.A + 10.RI.3.A

Grade 11

Analyze & Evaluate Quality

Analyze development of universal themes in American literature of different eras. Evaluate the relevance and quality of an author's premises, claims, and evidence.

11.RL.1.A + 11.RI.1.C

The inference progression is less about new skills appearing and more about the complexity of what students hold in mind while inferring. A 9th grader infers within a single text. A 10th grader infers across texts and cultural contexts. An 11th grader evaluates the quality of inferences and evidence — both the author's and their own.

Cognitive Handoff

Leaving 9th Grade

Draw conclusions from textual evidence that aren't directly stated. Distinguish between what a text says and what it implies. Begin recognizing that confusion often signals missing knowledge (background, vocabulary, structural) rather than inability to think.

The text doesn't say Odysseus is lying to the Phaeacians. But what evidence would you point to if you wanted to argue he might be? What makes you suspicious?

Leaving 10th Grade

Synthesize across sections, chapters, or paired texts to build an interpretation. Trace how evidence accumulates — not just finding one supporting quote but tracking a pattern across a full text. Evaluate whether evidence actually supports a claim or just seems related.

Victor says the Creature is evil. The Creature says he was made evil by abandonment. What evidence in the text supports each claim? Is there evidence that contradicts either?

Leaving 11th Grade

Evaluate the quality and sufficiency of evidence — both in the texts they read and in the arguments they construct. Hold competing interpretations and articulate what each explains well and what each misses. Recognize when an inference is well-supported versus when it's a reach.

Nick calls Gatsby "great." Is the novel agreeing with Nick, or is the novel showing us something about Nick by letting him use that word? What's the evidence for each reading?
In Our Texts

9th Grade

Romeo & Juliet

The play never says "the adults failed these children." But the evidence is everywhere — the Friar's plan is reckless, the Nurse abandons Juliet, the parents are consumed by the feud. A 9th grader who can gather that evidence and articulate the implied argument is doing inference work that builds toward the analytical capacity the later grades require.

10th Grade

Frankenstein

The novel never directly says "Victor is wrong." It gives you his version and the Creature's version and asks you to hold both and draw your own conclusion. The inference work is built into the structure — the nested narration forces the reader to evaluate competing accounts without the text resolving the tension for them.

11th Grade

The Great Gatsby

The final paragraph of Gatsby is one of the most analyzed passages in American literature — and it works entirely through inference. "So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past." The novel doesn't tell you what that means. It gives you an image, a rhythm, and a metaphor, and trusts you to bring everything you've learned across 180 pages to the act of interpretation. That's inference as the culminating reading skill.

Design Questions for Unit Planning

9th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

What does this text imply but never state — and is it something students could arrive at with the right question? The best inference work comes from moments where the text leaves a gap that's closeable with evidence and attention. Identify those gaps before the unit starts so you can design questions that lead students to them.

Where will students practice the difference between "what the text says" and "what the text means"? This distinction is the foundation of all analytical reading. It needs to be made visible and practiced repeatedly — not assumed.

How will you help students see that confusion is a signal to look harder, not a sign of failure? Many 9th graders stop reading when they're confused. The design move is normalizing "I don't understand this yet" as the beginning of inference, not the end of reading.

Depth anchor: Students gather evidence for the claim "the adults failed Romeo and Juliet" — a conclusion the play never states. The Friar's reckless plan, the Nurse's abandonment, the parents' blindness to the feud's cost. Students learn that an argument can be powerfully supported by the text even when the text never makes it explicitly. That's inference as evidence-building.

10th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Where does this text present competing accounts or perspectives that the reader must evaluate? Inference at the 10th grade level often means deciding what to believe when the text gives you conflicting information. Texts with unreliable narrators, competing perspectives, or ambiguous evidence are ideal.

How will students practice tracing evidence across a full text rather than finding a single supporting quote? The 10th grade shift is from "find evidence for this claim" to "track a pattern across the text and articulate what it reveals." That requires reading with accumulation in mind.

Where does the text leave something genuinely unresolved — and how will you design for students to sit with that rather than resolve it prematurely? Holding ambiguity is an inference skill. Texts that don't tell you the answer are teaching students that interpretation requires judgment, not just retrieval.

Depth anchor: Students evaluate Victor's claim that the Creature is evil against the Creature's claim that he was made evil by abandonment. They gather evidence for both, identify evidence that complicates both, and articulate what the novel leaves unresolved. The inference work IS the analytical work — there's no "right answer" to retrieve, only interpretations to build and defend.

11th Grade — When planning a unit, figure out:

Where will students evaluate the quality of evidence — both the author's and their own? The 11th grade move is metacognitive: not just making inferences but assessing whether those inferences are well-supported or whether they're reaching beyond what the text actually provides.

How will students hold competing interpretations and articulate what each one explains well and what each one misses? This is the highest-level inference work: recognizing that multiple readings can be valid but not equally well-supported, and having the tools to evaluate them.

Where does the text reward a second or third reading — where the first reading produces one inference and subsequent readings complicate or overturn it? Design for re-reading as an inference practice, not just a comprehension strategy.

Depth anchor: Students take a position on: "Nick calls Gatsby 'great.' Is the novel agreeing with Nick, or showing us something about Nick by letting him use that word?" They cite specific moments where Nick's narration supports or undermines his own assessment, and they evaluate the strength of each other's evidence. The conversation is about the quality of interpretive claims, not just their content.
